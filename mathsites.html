Cool Math Sites

Collected by Axel Boldt

Wolfram Alpha

Address: wolframalpha.com

Here you can solve all sorts of math problems. It's basically Mathematica on the web, but it's a lot more tolerant when it comes to syntax and will understand most inputs. Some examples:

·         Solve a differential equation: dy/dx = 2*x*y, y(0)=1

·         Plot functions in 2D or 3D: plot x^2*sin(xy), x=0..pi, y=0..pi

·         Eigenvalues of a matrix: eigenvalues of [[1,2,3],[2,1,1],[2,1,2]]

·         All sorts of constants: diameter of the Earth, or population of Africa

·         Many more examples: wolframalpha.com/examples/Math.html

MacTutor History of Mathematics

Address: www-history.mcs.st-and.ac.uk

A very comprehensive site on the history of mathematics. You can look up specific people by name or time period in the Biographies Index (Click on "Full MacTutor biography" once you have located a person), or trace the history of a mathematical concept with the History Topic Index. Great to prepare for giving a presentation.

The On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences

Address: oeis.org

This site provides the ultimate answer to questions of the form "What is the next term in the following sequence:…?" Once it has identified a sequence, it will provide comprehensive information about it, including definition, examples, references and code to compute it. This is very useful if you can solve small instances of a problem but you are looking for a general formula. It's best to enter about six terms or so.

If you don't know a term, you can also put in wildcards: "2, 7, 19, _, 111". To search for a sequence that contains the number 2, 7, 27 and 441 in any order and at any position, search for "2 7 27 441". More search tips: oeis.org/hints.html

If you can come up with an integer sequence that the system doesn't know, you can submit it yourself, and you will be credited. It is tough though.

Inverse symbolic calculator

Address: isc.carma.newcastle.edu.au/advanced

Suppose you have numerically computed some number, 1.64493406 say, and you would like to know if this is an approximation to any interesting expression. This site has the answer: it's actually π2/6. Wolfram Alpha can solve some of these problems, but this site is a lot more powerful.

Math Stackexchange

Address: math.stackexchange.com

A question-and-answer site for all things math-related. Questions and answers are ranked by usefulness. Before asking a question, you may want to search the site, since lots of questions have already been answered.

While Math Stackexchange is intended for people studying and using math, there's another site with the same format that is directed at researchers in mathematics: mathoverflow.net. This is hard-core and you won't be able to understand most questions or answers, but you can still get a feel for the kinds of problems professional mathematicians are working on.

MOOCs

Address: edx.org
Address: coursera.org
Address: udacity.com

A "MOOC" is a Massive Open Online Course: a free course that typically lasts 10-15 weeks and features weekly video lectures and online quizzes as well as exams. Instead of using a text book, many MOOCs use online notes. If you pass the final exam, you'll get a certificate (but usually no college credit). The math offerings include Statistics, Logic, College Algebra, Precalculus and Calculus I; there are also many courses in programming and computer science.
